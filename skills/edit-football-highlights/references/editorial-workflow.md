## Source indexing

Start with evidence from the actual file. Do not infer the source structure from its filename.

- Probe media metadata first.
- Make a coarse sheet at 20–30 second intervals.
- Make finer sheets at 5–10 second intervals around likely events.
- Record source timestamps, not only match-clock minutes.
- If commentary is useful, transcribe the final cut rather than the entire source whenever possible.

## Event selection

Keep an event when it advances at least one of these:

- stakes: teams, round, venue, score
- football: chance, save, tactical shift, goal
- conflict: foul, warning, dismissal, protest
- emotion: crowd response, celebration, disappointment
- closure: late pressure, final whistle, result

For each chance, normally keep:

- 2–6 seconds of buildup
- the touch, shot, save, or decision
- 2–5 seconds of reaction

Long disputes may be condensed into several short beats: trigger, referee decision, confrontation, attempted mediation, exit, restart.

## Story structure

Use a six-part arc for a single historic match:

1. **Identity** — teams, year, competition round.
2. **Promise** — lineups and first dangerous exchanges.
3. **Escalation** — increasingly physical or tactical pressure.
4. **Turning point** — dismissal, injury, controversy, or tactical change.
5. **Decision** — winning goal, decisive save, or shootout.
6. **Legacy** — result and why it mattered.

Do not overload the video with facts not visible or audible in the source. Verify any added historical claim before placing it on screen.

## Pacing defaults

- Open with a 4–7 second title.
- Keep lineup material to 10–25 seconds total.
- Use 8–25 second action clips; use longer clips only when continuity matters.
- Prefer hard cuts for archive sport. Reserve fades for opening and closing.
- Let the original crowd swell breathe around goals and major decisions.
- A 15-minute source usually yields a 4–7 minute recap.

## Historical-footage treatment

- Preserve black-and-white footage unless colorization is explicitly requested.
- Use restrained grain, vignette, and contrast; never bury the ball or player silhouettes.
- Avoid flashy social-media transitions on serious historical material.
- Use modern Chinese typography but period-appropriate restraint.
- Keep a small episode marker if the user is building a series.
